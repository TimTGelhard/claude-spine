# What Is This? (In Plain English)

> A guide for people who didn't grow up writing code.
> If "AI", "Claude", and "ChatGPT" all sound roughly the same to you — start here.

---

## The one-sentence version

This is a **handbook** that turns an AI coding assistant from a clever-but-clueless new hire into something closer to a **senior co-worker who remembers you, learns your habits, and gets better the longer you work together.**

---

## Start with the problem

You may have heard that AI can now "write software." It's true — but the reality is messier than the marketing.

Imagine you walked into your office and someone said:

> "Good news! I hired you a brilliant new assistant. They've read every book ever written. They never sleep. They work for almost nothing."

Sounds amazing. Then you meet them, and you discover:

- They have **no memory** of yesterday's conversation.
- They don't know **your company**, your **clients**, or the way **you** like things done.
- They're **eager to please** — so eager that if you ask a vague question, they'll just guess and act, instead of asking you back.
- They sometimes **make things up** very confidently.
- After a few hours of work, they get **tired and sloppy** without realizing it.

That is what working with AI coding tools is actually like out of the box. The intelligence is real. But without training, the *day-to-day* is exhausting — you spend half your time re-explaining things, double-checking, and cleaning up mistakes.

This product is the training, the handbook, and the memory — packaged together.

## The "it evolves" part

This is the piece most AI tools don't have, and it's what makes this worth real money.

Every time you work with it, the AI quietly watches for **patterns and moments worth remembering**:

- You corrected it the same way twice
- A new shortcut would have saved you 20 minutes
- A trick worked great and should become standard
- A mistake almost happened — write down how to avoid it

It doesn't act on these immediately. It writes them into a **suggestions list**. Later, when you have a quiet moment, you tell it: *"show me what you've learned."*

## A small day-to-day example

Here's what this looks like in practice, without any technical jargon.

**Without the product:**

> You: "Build me a contact form."
> AI: *(writes 200 lines of code, picks a colour scheme, sends emails to a service you don't have an account with, forgets to make it work on phones)*
> You: *(spend 40 minutes cleaning up)*

**With the product:**

> You: "Build me a contact form."
> AI: "Before I start — three quick questions: where should the messages go, do you want it to work in Dutch as well as English, and is this for the bakery website or the new one? I noticed last time you wanted forms to look like the example on page 3 of your portfolio. Should I reuse that?"
> You: *(answer in 30 seconds, AI builds the right thing the first time)*

It's the difference between a contractor who needs a manager and one who manages themselves.

## Why this didn't exist before

Until recently, AI assistants were too small and too forgetful to make this worth doing. The model would forget halfway through a single task — there was no point teaching it your habits, because it couldn't hold onto them.

In 2026, that changed. The new generation of AI can read **a small library** in one sitting and remember it. Suddenly the bottleneck isn't memory — it's **what you put in front of it.** Most people are still pointing it at a blank page. This product is the opposite: a structured, personal, evolving foundation that grows with the user.

It's not a smarter AI. It's the *manual* the AI was missing.
